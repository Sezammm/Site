So, living in Pattaya, I had grown a bit shaggy, and the day before this little adventure my coworker Sergey recommended a barbershop not far from my workplace.

For some reason there was only one barber in the shop - a middle-aged Thai man. Maybe his colleagues were off that day, or maybe he was a one-man army. I never quite figured that out.

After greeting me, he pointed to where I should sit. There were three empty chairs to choose from, so availability was not the problem.

The barbershop itself was a room of about 16 square meters. It did not look especially fresh, but it was perfectly decent: mirrors everywhere - three large ones and three smaller ones behind the chairs; three heavy metal barber chairs, slightly worn out but still soft and covered in faux leather; and a long wooden bench stretching along the wall with an older gentleman sitting on it.

As it turned out, that gentleman was a plainclothes police officer. Nice enough fellow, very good-natured. We chatted a bit about the weather, and about how winters in Siberia are very, very cold - which, to be fair, is not exactly breaking news, but still made for polite international conversation.

Now, back to the haircut :)

The haircut itself was priced at 100 baht. Since I had come during lunch break and still had a long workday ahead of me, I asked him to wash my hair afterward as well, so that the freshly cut hair would not fly around my office like tiny snowflakes and irritate my coworker with their free-spirited behavior.

That cost another 100 baht, bringing the grand total to 200 baht.

Once I was seated, the barber got down to business. Before starting, he asked whether I wanted him to trim my mustache and beard too. At that time I was fairly bearded and mustached, let us put it that way :)

After a moment’s thought, I declined. A haircut and hair wash would be enough. He went over my head with clippers from different angles, sprayed and wet my hair, worked with scissors, and in general did everything quickly and neatly.

I actually liked the result more than the time I had my hair cut by a Thai woman in a salon on Chaiyaphruek.

When he finished, he wrapped another towel around my shoulders, brought a smaller towel, folded it several times - let us call this mysterious engineering solution “the little folded thing” - put a cloth napkin on top of it, covered my temples and neck with soapy solution, and then stepped away.

Watching him suspiciously in the mirror, I noticed that he was now holding a straight razor with a shiny blade. A thought flashed through my head: “Well... I hope I am in good hands. I would rather not spend the rest of the day walking around with decorative cuts on my neck.”

He came back and very carefully shaved off the remaining little bristles sticking out like tree stumps after a lumberjack had been through the forest. I must admit, during this procedure I felt both nervous and oddly pleased at the same time. Goosebumps were running all over my body.

I had already sighed with relief, but then he suddenly took that little folded thing and placed it under my nose, right against my upper lip. I honestly had no idea what masterpiece he was preparing next, but I decided not to interrupt the artist and simply waited.

With one smooth movement he pulled out a pair of small scissors and went straight into my nostrils!!! That turn of events I absolutely did not see coming. Still, I decided to let the experiment continue :) He carefully trimmed the “extra” nose hairs - even though I had not asked for that service.

After that, I also could not predict his next move. What “instrument of torture” do you think he produced? Cotton swabs!

I did not actually see them, but judging by the feeling, they were cotton swabs - or at least cotton wrapped around something thin. One by one, into each of my ears, he began pushing and cleaning my ear canals. Apparently he decided that I was not hearing well enough and chose to help.

I lay there like a log and had already reached a philosophical state where I no longer cared what exactly was being done to my body. It was unusual, and I tried not to show that inside I was completely amazed by the whole situation :)

Next came the actual hair wash. Before that, he stuffed earplugs into the ears he had just carefully cleaned - apparently so that his work would not go to waste - wrapped my shoulders with yet another towel, and then brought some strange thick collar-like thing to keep the water off me.

He spun the chair around and lowered it into a horizontal position so that my head was over the sink. Then he started washing my hair: shampoo, a good thorough scrub, rinse - and he repeated the process three times.

Perhaps not all three rounds were shampoo; maybe one of them was conditioner or something else equally luxurious. In any case, three applications of washing liquid and three rinses. Very serious head maintenance.

He squeezed the water out of my hair, turned the chair back around - still in a horizontal position - dried my hair with a towel, and then began delivering a light, completely undeclared relaxing massage.

He kneaded my head, arms, wrists, hands; pulled my arms back; massaged my shoulders; moved on to my back; and then, with some determination, twisted my spine one way and the other. I must admit, it was very pleasant.

After all that, he said something like “Kaaap” - basically meaning, “Please,” or rather, “Your haircut is done!” For all his effort I did not hesitate to leave a tip: 20 baht on top of the 200. He was delighted, glowing all over, and invited me to come back again next time.

So that was Thai service.
